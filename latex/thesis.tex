    The aim of this work is to study the \textit{branched optimal transport} problem, a variation of the optimal transport problem.

    The first mathematical formulation of an \textit{optimal transport} problem was given by Gaspard Monge in 1781~\cite[Monge]{monge1781mémoire}.
    In his work, Monge considered the problem of moving a pile of soil from one location to another (in fact, a special case of the problem is known as the \textit{Earth-mover distance}).
    The modern formulation of the problem is due to Kantorovich~\cite[Kantorovich 1958]{Kantorovich1958}, almost two centuries later.
    The language used by Kantorovich is that of \textit{measure theory} and formally the problem is stated as follows:
    given $X \subset \R^d$, two probability measures $\mu_0, \mu_1$ on $X$ with finite and equal mass, a cost function $c: X \times X \to \R^+$ find a transport map $T: X \to X$ that realizes the following infimum:
    \begin{equation}
        \inf \left\{ \int_X c(x, T(x)) d\mu_0(x) \, : \, T_\# \mu_0 = \mu_1 \right\}
        \label{eq:monge}
    \end{equation}

    Intuitively the cost function $c(x,y)$ represents the cost of moving a unit mass from $x$ to $y$, and the functional to be minimized represents is the sum (integral)
    over all the particles that need to be moved. Importantly, the particles do not interact with each other and there is no notion of \textit{path} involved in the transport.
    This formulation is very generic and flexible: many authors and studies consider restrictions with extra assumptions (e.g. $c$ is a distance function, $X$ is a compact/convex set, etc.)
    while other study even more general settings (e.g. $X$ is a generic metric space).
    Nevertheless, all of the above choices lead to interesting paths that will be briefly explored in the literature review (todo: ha senso dirlo?)
    and this work will also juggle between different assumptions, particularly in the computational study where the problem is restricted to atomic measures.

    There are two ways to formulate the problem, called \textit{Lagrangian} and \textit{Eulerian} formulations, which are equivalent in the sense that they yield the same optimal transport plan
    when it exists. The Lagrangian formulation uses more basic mathematical machinery and is more intuitive, while the Eulerian formulation can leverage more advanced tools from geometric measure theory.

    \subsubsection{Lagrangian formulation}

    The Lagrangian formulation, intuitively, works as follows: it first considers all the possible 1-Lipschitz paths which are eventually constant with domain in $X \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ and assigns
    a measure of probability on those paths. The idea is that each of those paths is a possible way to transport a unit of mass and assigning a mass $\lambda$ to a path $\gamma$
    means that $\lambda$ units of mass are transported along that path. In the case of non-atomic starting and ending measures, no single path
    has a positive mass, but rather a density (a measure) on the space of paths is defined. A measure on the space of paths is said to be a transport plan
    for given $\mu^+$ and $\mu^-$ if a condition on the push-forward of the starting and stopping time of the paths is satisfied. This push-forward operation
    can be seen as measuring how much mass ends in a certain point $x$ of the space $X$ after the transport.

    Formally, given $X \subset \mathbb{R}^N$ compact, $K$ the space of 1-Lipschitz paths which are eventually constant on $X$, and
    two measures of equal mass $\mu^+$ and $\mu^-$ on $X$, a traffic plan is a measure $\mathbf{P}$ on $K$ that transports $\mu^+$ to $\mu^-$.

    In order to define what "transport" means, consider the maps $\pi_0, \pi_\infty : K \rightarrow X$ given by $\pi_0(\gamma) = \gamma(0)$ and $\pi_\infty(\gamma) = \gamma(T(\gamma))$, where $T(\gamma)$
    is the stopping time of the path $\gamma$.
    $\mathbf{P}$ is a traffic plan if:
    \[
        \mu^+ = (\pi_0)_{\#}\mathbf{P}, \quad \mu^- = (\pi_\infty)_{\#}\mathbf{P}
    \]

    According to Skorokhod theorem~\cite[Theorem A.3]{Bernot}, any traffic plan $\mathbf{P}$ can be parameterized by a measurable function
    $\chi : \Omega = [0, |\Omega|] \rightarrow K$, where $\Omega$ is a measure space which is identified with an interval, such that
    $\mathbf{P}= \chi_{\#} \lambda$, where $\lambda$ is the Lebesgue measure on $[0, |\Omega|]$.

    $(\omega, t)$.

    With this formulation, the \textbf{energy} (or cost) of a parameterized traffic plan is defined as:
    \begin{equation}
        E^{\alpha}(\mathbf{P}) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^N} |x|_\mathbf{P}^{\alpha} d \mathcal{H}^1(x)
        \label{eq:energy}
    \end{equation}

    where $|x|_\mathbf{P}$ is the multiplicity of the point defined as:
    \begin{equation}
        |x|_\mathbf{P} = | \{\omega: x \in \chi(\omega, \mathbb{R}) \} |
    \end{equation}

    \subsubsection{Eulerian formulation}

    An equivalent formulation of the problem has been proposed by~\cite{paolini2006optimal}, using the language of 
    geometric measure theory~\cite{simon2014introduction} \cite{federer2014geometric}.
    \begin{definition}
        \textbf{Currents and graphs definition} 
        A transport path is a 1-rectifiable current T whose boundary $\partial T = \mu^+ - \mu^-$. This can be identified with a vector valued
        measure $T = \vec{T}(\theta \mathcal{H}^1 \llcorner E)$ where $\vec{T}$ is a vector field with norm 1, $E$ is a set of finite $\mathcal{H}^1$ measure and
        $\theta$ is a scalar field.
        The energy of a current is defined as:
        \begin{equation}
            \mathcal{M}^{\alpha}(T) \coloneqq \int_{E} \theta(x)^\alpha \, d\mathcal{H}^1(x)
        \end{equation}

        Here $E$ is contained in a countable union of curves of class $C^1$.
    \end{definition}

    The equivalence of this formulation is summarized in \cite[Chapter 9]{Bernot} and first proved in \cite{paolini2006optimal}.
    In particular any normal one-dimensional current with compact support can be represented as a traffic plan and vice versa.
    This implies that both minimization problems attain the same minimum value and that there is a one-to-one correspondence between the minimizers.

    \begin{definition}
        \textbf{Loop-free}
        A traffic plan $\mathbf{P}$ is loop-free if there is a prametrization $\chi$ of $\mathbf{P}$ such that for almost every $\omega$
        the path $\chi(\omega)$ is injective on $[0, T(\chi(\omega))]$.
    \end{definition}

    \begin{definition}
        Let $\chi$ be a loop-free traffic plan, so that $t_x(\omega) := \chi^{-1}(\omega, \cdot)(x)$ is well defined.
        Let $x, y$ in $S_\chi$. Define
        \[
            \Omega_{\overset{\rightarrow}{x}y} := \{\omega \in \Omega_x^\chi \cap \Omega_y^\chi : t_x(\omega) < t_y(\omega)\},
        \]
        the set of fibers passing through $x$ and then through $y$.
        The restriction of $\chi$ to $\bigcup_{\omega \in \Omega_{\overset{\rightarrow}{x}y}} \{\omega\} \times [t_x(\omega), t_y(\omega)]$ is denoted by $\chi_{xy}$.
        It is the traffic plan made of all pieces of fibers of $\chi$ joining $x$ to $y$. Denote its support by $\Gamma^{xy} = S_{\chi_{xy}}$.

    \end{definition}

    \section{Novel theoretical results}

    In this section, the main results are presented, which provide lower bounds on the angles between edges.
    These bounds depend either on the specific instance of the problem $(\mu^{\pm}, N, \alpha)$ or only on the pair $(N, \alpha)$.
    Once these bounds are obtained, the results for spherical cap packing~\ref{definition:spherical_cap_results}
    can be used to derive upper bounds on the number of branches in the optimal transport problem.

    The following lemma offers an improvement over~\cite[Lemma 12.16]{Bernot},
    where the competitor is considered along the edge instead of the span defined by the three vertices.

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lemma:bound_opposite_direction}
        Consider a vertex $s$ and two terminals $s_1, s_2$ connected to it (as illustrated in \ref{fig:angle_bound_example_inverted}). Let $m_1, m_2$ be the masses of the two edges connecting
        $s$ to $s_1, s_2$ respectively, such that $m_1 \ge m_2$ and $m_1$ is flowing toward $s$ and $m_2$ is flowing away from $s$.
        Let $\theta$ be the angle between the two edges. Then, for every $\alpha \in (0,1]$:

        \begin{equation}
            \cos(\theta) \le \frac{(m_1 - m_2) ^ {2\alpha} - m_1^{2\alpha} - m_2^{2\alpha}}{2m_2^\alpha m_1^\alpha}
        \end{equation}

        \begin{proof}
            It can be assumed that $p_1$ and $p_2$ lie on the unit circle by zooming in and then rescaling, using the property that every restriction of an optimal
            solution is optimal~\cite[Chapter 8]{Bernot}. Denote the candidate new branching point by $x$ and set $s=(0,0)$.
            The cost $f$ of this configuration of the tree can be written as:
            \begin{equation}
                f(x) = m_1^{\alpha}\|p_1 - x \| + m_2^{\alpha} \|p_2 - x\| + (m_1-m_2)^{\alpha} \|x\|
            \end{equation}
            By setting $x = \epsilon v$ with $\|v\|=1$ and expanding to the first order around $\epsilon=0$:
            \begin{equation}
                f(\epsilon v) = f(0) - \epsilon m_1^{\alpha} v \cdot p_1 - \epsilon m_2^{\alpha} v \cdot p_2 + (m_1-m_2)^{\alpha} \epsilon + o(\epsilon)
            \end{equation}

            Since the interest is in checking whether $f(0)$ is not a maximum, it suffices to consider values of $\epsilon$ close to $0$.

            \begin{figure}[h]
                \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{circle}
                \centering
                \caption{Diagram of $p_1$, $p_2$ and $s$ for edges flowing in opposite directions}
            \end{figure}

            The vector $v$ can be parametrized with the angle $\beta$ from $p_1$ (going toward $p_2$), leading to the following equation:
            \begin{equation}
                f(\epsilon) - f(0) = \epsilon \left[ (m_1-m_2)^\alpha - m_2^\alpha \cos\beta - m_1^\alpha (\cos\theta\cos\beta - \sin\theta\sin\beta) \right] + o(\epsilon)
            \end{equation}

            To prove that $\theta$ is not optimal, there are two options:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item prove that there exists $\beta$ such that the first-order coefficient is negative
                \item prove that the first-order coefficient is greater than $0$ for all $\beta$
            \end{itemize}

            To this end, fix $\theta, m_1, m_2$ and study the minimum of the function:
            \begin{equation}
                (m_1-m_2)
                ^\alpha - m_2^\alpha \cos\beta - m_1^\alpha (\cos\theta\cos\beta - \sin\theta\sin\beta)
            \end{equation}

            Letting $t = \cos(\beta)$, the following equation must have a solution for $\beta$:
            \begin{equation}
                \label{eq:optimality_condition_proof}
                (m_1-m_2)
                ^{\alpha} + \cos\beta(-m_2^\alpha-m_1^\alpha t) + \sin\beta(m_1^\alpha \sqrt{1-t^2}) = 0
            \end{equation}

            Finding a value of $\beta$ such that this equation evaluates to 0 is not sufficient.
            It is also necessary to verify whether this point corresponds to a local minimum.

            It is known that there exists some $\beta$ for which this expression becomes negative (by choosing $\beta$ outside the
            span of $p_1$ and $p_2$, which contradicts the convex hull property of the optimal solution). Therefore, if $\theta$
            is not optimal, this function must cross zero.

            By solving equation~\ref{eq:optimality_condition_proof}, using that $c^2 \le a^2 + b^2$ is a necessary and
            sufficient condition for an equation of the form $a\cos\beta + b\sin\beta = c$ to have a solution, the following condition is obtained:
            \begin{equation}
                \label{eq:optimality_condition_proof_2}
                t \ge \frac{(m_1-m_2)^{2\alpha} - m_1^{2\alpha} - m_2^{2\alpha}}{2m_2^\alpha m_1^\alpha}
            \end{equation}
        \end{proof}
    \end{lemma}
    
        It is observed that~\ref{eq:optimality_condition_proof_2} is almost the same as~\cite[Lemma 12.2]{Bernot}.
    In fact, the only difference is that along the edge connecting $s$ to the branching point, the difference
    of the masses appears instead of the sum.

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lemma:difference_bound_decreasing}
        The function:
        \begin{equation}
            f(m_1, m_2) = \frac{(m_1 - m_2) ^ {2\alpha} - m_1^{2\alpha} - m_2^{2\alpha}}{2m_2^\alpha m_1^\alpha}
        \end{equation}
        is decreasing in the ratio $\frac{m_2}{m_1}$ and tends to $0$ as $\frac{m_2}{m_1} \rightarrow 0$.

        \begin{proof}
            The function $f$ is homogeneous and can therefore be reparametrized in terms of the ratio of the masses. Letting $m = \frac{m_2}{m_1}$,
            one obtains:
            \begin{equation}
                f(m_1, m_2) = g(m) \coloneqq \frac{(1 - m) ^ {2\alpha} - 1 - m^{2\alpha}}{2m^\alpha}
            \end{equation}
            It can then be verified that this function is decreasing in $m$, since $(1-m)^{2\alpha}$, $-m^{2\alpha}$,
            and $1/ m^\alpha$ are all decreasing functions.

            Therefore,
            \begin{equation}
                \sup_{m_1, m_2} f(m_1,m_2) = \lim_{m \rightarrow 0} g(m) = 0
            \end{equation}

            To compute the limit, de l'Hôpital's rule is applied:
            \begin{equation}
                \lim_{m \rightarrow 0} g(m) = \lim_{m \rightarrow 0} \frac{-2\alpha (1-m)^{2\alpha-1} - 2\alpha m^{2\alpha-1}}{2\alpha m^{\alpha-1}} = 0
            \end{equation}
        \end{proof}

    \end{lemma}



    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lemma:bound_general_direction}

        Let $\mathbf{P}$ be an optimal traffic plan transporting $\mu^+$ to $\mu^-$ with finite energy and finite graph structure.
        Consider a branching point $s$ located outside the support of $\mu^{\pm}$, with two branches connecting $s$ to points $x_1$ and $x_2$.
        Denoting the angle $\angle x_1 s x_2$ by $\theta$, the following inequality holds:

        \begin{equation}
            \label{eq:bound_general_direction}
            \cos(\theta) \le \max\left( 2^{2\alpha - 1} - 1, 0 \right).
        \end{equation}

        Furthermore, the degree is bounded by $C \left( \frac{\theta_{\max}}{2}, N \right)$, where $\theta_{\max}$
        is the angle that realizes the maximum in equation~\ref{eq:bound_general_direction}.

        \begin{proof}
            By combining lemmas~\ref{lemma:bound_opposite_direction} and~\ref{lemma:difference_bound_decreasing}, along with~\cite[Lemma 12.5]{Bernot}, one can obtain a bound on $\cos \theta$ that applies to all masses and depends only on $\alpha$.

            Taking the supremum over all masses, the conclusion is that for a three-point system, there is no branching point if
            \begin{equation}
                \cos(\theta) \le \max \left( \max\left( 2^{2\alpha - 1} - 1, 0 \right), 0 \right).
            \end{equation}
        \end{proof}

    \end{lemma}

    \begin{corollary}
        In the case where $\mu^+$ and $\mu^-$ are two measures with finite support it is known that lemma~\ref{lemma:bound_general_direction}
        holds also for points in their support.
    \end{corollary}

    \subsection{Uniform bound on length of fibers}

    As mentioned in the open problems section of~\cite[Chapter 15, question 13]{Bernot}, one of the open questions is whether the length of the fibers
    $\chi(\omega, \cdot)$ is uniformly bounded. The first step is to prove this result for fixed $\mu^\pm$ and then extend to a uniform bound over all the possible measures.
    This section is dedicated to an attempt to prove this result: the only conclusion drawn is that the length of the fibers is uniformly bounded if along any 
    trajectory the density of the fiber decreases at most polynomially. This is quite informative as it shows that the hard part of the problem regards only the instances
    where the density decays faster than polynomially, which also imply that fibers over some length $L$ do not contribute much to the cost of the optimal solution.

    The proof is based on a contradiction argument: we assume that there exists a fiber with arbitrarily large length and show the existence of a better competitor.
    TODO: include picture of the taglio and cucito technique.

    What we want to show is that, for fixed $\alpha$, $\mu^+$ and $\mu^-$, the optimal transport parametrization $\chi$
    is equibounded in the sense that there is no path $\gamma$ that is non self-intersecting and such that $|\gamma(t)|_\chi$ is decreasing and positive
    for every $t$.
    The idea of the proof is to assume that there is such a path and show that it is not optimal by finding a better competitor.
    Without loss of generality, we can assume that $\gamma$ is parametrized by arc length and that $\gamma(0) = 1$.
    We define a competitor $\tilde\chi$ as the union of two paths, $\tilde{\gamma}$ and $\tilde{\psi}$, defined as follows:
    \begin{align*}
        |\tilde{\gamma}(t)| &= |\gamma(t)| - |\gamma(K)| \quad \text{for } t \in [0, K] \\
        |\tilde{\gamma}(t)| &= |\gamma(t)| \quad \text{for } t \in [K, \infty) \\
        \tilde{\gamma}(t) &= \gamma(t) \quad \text{for } t \in [0, \infty) \\
        |\tilde{\psi}(t)| &= \gamma(K) \\
        \tilde{\psi}(t) &= \text{Straight line from } \gamma(0) \text{ to } \gamma(K)
    \end{align*}

    Basically, we cut from the beginning of $\gamma$ the part that is longer than $|\gamma(K)|$ and we add a straight line from
    $\gamma(0)$ to $\gamma(K)$. We can arbitrarily start from 0 as we can just shift the path to the right and look at the restriction,
    rescaling it so that it has mass 1.

    We can now compute the cost of $\tilde{\chi}$:

    \begin{align*}
        E^\alpha(\tilde{\chi}) - E^\alpha(\chi) &= \int_0^K |\psi(t)|^\alpha \psi'(t) \, dt - \int_0^K \left[\gamma(t) - \gamma(K)\right]^\alpha \, dt \\
        &\quad - \int_0^K \gamma(t)^\alpha \, dt \\
        &\le |\gamma(K)|^\alpha diam(X) +  \int_0^K \left[\gamma(t) - \gamma(K)\right]^\alpha \, dt \\
        &\quad - \int_0^K \gamma(t)^\alpha \, dt \\
    \end{align*}

    We can now use the concavity of the function $x \mapsto x^\alpha$ to expand to the first order the second term in the right hand side:

    \begin{equation}
        \int_0^K \left[\gamma(t) - \gamma(K)\right]^\alpha \, dt \le \int_0^K \gamma(t)^\alpha - \alpha \gamma(t)^{\alpha - 1} \gamma(K) \, dt
    \end{equation}

    \subsection{Discrete branched transport}
    \label{sec:DBT}

    The Discrete Branched Transport (DBT) is a special case of branched transport
    where the measures $\mu^+$ and $\mu^-$ are finite and atomic.

    Formally, we can define the DBT as follows: 
    Given a dimension $N$, a cost parameter $\alpha$, and a set of terminal points $P = \{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^N$ with
    corresponding masses $M = \{m_1, \ldots, m_n\}$, such that:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i = 0$
        \item $P^+$ is the set of terminals with positive mass (sources) and $P^-$ is the set of terminals with negative mass (sinks)
    \end{itemize}

    the goal is to find a weighted graph $(S \cup P, E, f)$, where $S = \{s_1, \ldots, s_m\}$
    is the set of additional points
    $s_i \in \R^N$, $E \subseteq S \cup P \times S \cup P$ is the set of edges and
    $f : E \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is the flow on each edge.

    Borrowing the therminology of Euclidean Steiner trees, we call $S$ the Steiner points and $P$ the terminals.

    The graph must form a network flow with sources ($P^-$) and sinks ($P^+$) at the terminal points in accordance with their masses,
    such that the following cost function is minimized:
    \[
        \sum_{(x,y) \in E} f_{xy}^\alpha \|x - y\|
    \]
    \begin{remark}
        It follows from~\ref{lemma:loop_free} that the optimal solution of a DBT problem is a union of trees.
        In the special case where there is only one source then the optimal solution is a tree becuase of the connectivity
        of the solution.
    \end{remark}
    \begin{remark}
        The problem remains unchanged if the signs of the masses are inverted, meaning that sources and sinks can be swapped.
        Therefore, it is sufficient to analyze either the case of a single source or a single sink, as they are the same problem.
    \end{remark}

    \subsection{DBT Formulation}

    In this section a formulation for the DBT problem with a cost parameter $\alpha$ and a
    single source $p^-=\{p_0\}$ based on~\cite[MMX]{mmx} is presented.
    The formulation is a MINLP problem with both binary variables and continuous variables.

    \begin{align}
        \text{(DBT)}: \; \min &\sum_{[i,j] \in E_1} f_{ij}^{\alpha}\|p_i - x^j\| + \sum_{[i,j] \in E_2} f_{ji}^{\alpha}\|x^i - x^j\| \\
        & \sum_{j \in S} y_{ij} = 1 \quad \text{for } i \in P \\
        & \sum_{k < j, k \in S} y_{kj} = 1 \quad \text{for } j \in S - \{p+1\}, \label{dbt_form:connectedness} \\
        & \sum_{i \in S} f_{ji} = m_j \quad \text{for } j \in P^+ \label{dbt_form:sum_sinks} \\
        & \sum_{i \in S} f_{ji} = -m_j \quad \text{for } j \in P^- \label{dbt_form:sum_sources} \\
        & \sum_{i \in P} f_{ij} + \sum_{k < j, k \in S} f_{kj} + \sum_{k > j, k \in S} f_{jk} = 0 \quad \text{for } j \in S \label{dbt_form:sum_steiner_0}  \\
        & \sum_{i \in P} y_{ij} + \sum_{k < j, k \in S} y_{kj} + \sum_{k > j, k \in S} y_{jk} = 3 \quad \text{for } j \in S \label{dbt_form:degree_bound} \\
        & f_{ij} \le y_{ij} \quad \text{for } [i,j] \in E \\
        & s_i = \sum_{j \in P} c_{ij} p_j \\
        & y_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}, \quad [i, j] \in E, \quad x^i \in \mathbb{R}^n, \quad i \in S \\
        & f_{ij} \in [0,1] \quad \text{for } [i,j] \in E\\
        & c_{ij} \in \R_+ \quad i \in S, \quad j \in P
    \end{align}
    where the following notation is used:

    \begin{itemizeb}
        \leftskip 0.8 cm\labelsep=.3 cm
        \item [$P$] is the set of terminals $\{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$,
        \item [$S$] is the set of Steiner $\{s_1, \ldots\}$ points (indexed as $S = \{|P| + 1, \dots, 2|P|-2\}$),
        \item [$E_1$] is the set of edges connecting terminals to Steiner points, such that $(i,j) \in E_1$ when $i \in P$ and $j \in S$.,
        \item [$E_2$] is the set of edges connecting Steiner points, such that $(i,j) \in E_2$ if $i,j \in S$ and $i < j$.
    \end{itemizeb}

    \begin{itemizeb}
        \leftskip 0.8 cm\labelsep=.3 cm
        \item[$\alpha \in (0,1)$] is the cost parameter
        \item [$M$] is the set of masses $\{m_1, \ldots, m_n\}$ of the terminals
        \item [$f_{ij}$] flow on the edge $(i, j) \in E$ going from $j$ to $i$  {TODO: controlla che sia corretta la direzione}
        \item [$y_{ij}$] binary variable indicating the presence of an un-oriented edge connecting $i$ to $j$.
    \end{itemizeb}


    \subsection{Proof of correctness}

    Correctness here refers to the ability to reconstruct one of the optimal solutions to the DBT problem from an optimal solution of the DBT formulation. 
    Accordingly, a function $\phi$ is constructed to map feasible solutions of the formulation into feasible solutions of DBT (i.e., graphs), 
    and it must be proven that optimality is preserved.

    Outline of the proof:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item construction of the map that takes feasible solutions to graphs
        \item proof that the objective functions are the same
        \item proof of optimality for connected optimal solution where terminals are leaves and branching points have degree 3
        \item dropping the assumption that terminals are leaves
        \item general proof
    \end{enumerate}

    \subsection*{Step 1}
    A function $\phi$ is defined that maps $\mathbb{R}^{N (|P|-2)} \times \{0,1\}^{|E|} \times \R^{|E|}$ to an oriented weighted graph in $\mathbb{R}^N$ as follows:

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item For each $i \in P$, a terminal is added at $p_i$.
        \item For each $j \in S$, a vertex $s_j \in \R^N$ with coordinates $x^j$ is added.
        \item Multiple vertices with the same coordinates are collapsed into one and identified with any of the indices.
        \item For each $[i,j] \in E$ with $y_{ij} = 1$, an edge is added between $v_i$ and $v_j$.
        \item The flow $f_{ij}$ is assigned to the edge connecting $v_i$ to $v_j$. Note that either $(i,j) \in E$ or $(j,i) \in E$, but not both, so flows cannot cancel.
    \end{enumerate}

    This function is not injective, as permuting the indices of the Steiner points yields the same graph.
    It is first shown that $\phi(\cdot)$ is a feasible solution of DBT. Since it is a graph, it remains to verify that the flow constraints hold.

    The (only) source is a special terminal, constrained to be connected to the first Steiner point (index $|P|+1$) due to~\ref{dbt_form:sum_sources}.

    The constraints form a tree with leaves as sink terminals, leading to all flows going "downstream" in any optimal solution. 
    This is consistent with the topological sorting of the tree indices imposed by~\ref{dbt_form:connectedness}.
    For any terminal $p$, in $\phi(GMMX)$ a (possibly empty) set of degenerate Steiner points indexed by $S_p = \{s_1, \dots, s_m\}$ 
    may exist at the same coordinates.
    The total flow of the collapsed vertex is:
    \begin{equation}
        \sum_{i \in S_p} \left[ \sum_{j<i}^{f_{ji}} - \sum_{j>i}^{f_{ij}}   \right] + \sum_{j \in S} f_{pj} = m_p
    \end{equation}
    By constraints~\ref{dbt_form:sum_steiner_0} and~\ref{dbt_form:sum_sinks}, the final flow is exactly $m_p$.

    The same construction applies for coinciding Steiner points that do not coincide with any terminal, ensuring flow conservation.

    It has thus been shown that the set of feasible solutions of the formulation is a subset of the set of feasible solutions of DBT via $\phi$.
    Therefore, to establish equivalence, it remains to show that for any optimal solution in DBT, a corresponding optimal solution of the formulation exists that maps to it.

    \subsection*{Step 2}
    Denote the feasible regions of the MINLP formulation and DBT problem by $Formulations$ and $Graphs$ respectively. 
    The following diagram is shown to commute:
    \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
            & \mathbb{R}  \\
            Formulations \arrow[ur, "Obj"] \arrow[rr, "\phi"'] & & Graphs  \arrow[ul, "Obj"]
        \end{tikzcd}
    \]

    The objective function in the formulation is the sum of $(f_{ij} + f_{ji})^\alpha$, which, after applying $\phi$, becomes the flow 
    along the edge $(i,j)$, weighted by $\| p_i - x^j \|$ or $\|x^i-x^j \|$, representing Euclidean distances between Steiner points and terminals.
    The fact that there could be a degeneracy is not important as those points have $0$ distance and do not contribute to the
    objective value.

    \subsection*{Step 3}

    It is now shown that a feasible solution to the MNILP exists that maps to a connected optimal solution of DBT where terminals are 
    leaves and branching points have degree 3.
    For each Steiner point $s_i$ in the graph, set $x^i = s_i$. Set $y_{ij} = 1$ and re-order the Steiner points 
    such that their indices are topologically sorted with respect to the tree structure of the graph.
    Then set $f_{ij}$ or $f_{ji}$ to the appropriate value for each edge $(i,j)$ in the optimal DBT solution.
    The degree constraints are not violated since the mapping originates from an optimal DBT solution.
    An edge counting argument, leveraging the tree structure, shows that there are enough Steiner points to connect all terminals:
    \begin{equation}
        |S| + |P| - 1 = \frac{1}{2} \left( 3|S| + \sum_{i \in P} d(i) \right)
    \end{equation}
    Which implies:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:edge_counting}
        \sum_{i \in P} d(i) = 2|P| - 2 - |S|
    \end{equation}

    Given the assumption $d(i)=1$, this holds.
    To satisfy the connectivity constraint~\ref{dbt_form:connectedness}, Steiner points must be re-ordered in a topological order, which exists since the graph is a tree.

    \subsection*{Step 4}
    The case where terminals can have degree greater than $1$, while assuming branching points have degree $3$, is now addressed.
    The strategy is to add degenerate Steiner points to the graph until all terminals become leaves.
    During this process, the number of added Steiner points must be tracked.
    From equation~\ref{eq:edge_counting}, it follows that for each "extra" degree, one additional Steiner point is available.
    The procedure is as follows:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Consider a terminal $p_i$ with degree $d(i)$ connected to points $\{x_1, \dots, x_{d(i)}\}$
        \item Add $d(i) - 1$ degenerate Steiner points $\{s_1, \dots, s_{d(i)-1}\}$ at the same coordinates as $p_i$
        \item Connect them with edges $\{ (p_i, s_1), (s_1, s_2), \dots, (s_{d(i)-1}, x_{d(i)}) \}$
        \item Also connect edges $\{ (s_i, x_i) \}$ for $i = 1, \dots, d(i)-1$
    \end{enumerate}
    This preserves the connectedness and topology of the graph. The procedure can be repeated until all terminals have degree $1$.
    Once the topology is fixed, flows are uniquely determined by the masses on the terminals. 
    Re-ordering the Steiner points may be necessary to satisfy the connectivity constraint~\ref{dbt_form:connectedness}.

    \subsection*{Step 5}

    As in step 4, for each branching point with degree $d(i)$, $d(i) - 3$ degenerate Steiner points can be added and connected in a chain. 
    Re-ordering the Steiner points ensures compliance with the connectivity constraint~\ref{dbt_form:connectedness}.
    According to~\ref{eq:edge_counting}, a sufficient number of Steiner points is available to apply this procedure to every branching point.
    \qed

        & s_i = \sum_{j \in P} c_{ij} p_j \\
        & y_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}, \quad [i, j] \in E, \quad s^i \in \mathbb{R}^n, \quad i \in S \\
        & c_{ij} \in \R_+ \quad i \in S, \quad j \in P,
    \end{align}

    Where:
    \begin{itemizeb}
        \leftskip 0.8 cm\labelsep=.3 cm
        \item [$P$] is the set of terminals $\{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$,
        \item [$S$] is the set of Steiner $\{s_1, \ldots\}$ points (indexed as $S = \{|P| + 1, \dots, 2|P|-2\}$),
        \item [$E_1$] is the set of edges connecting terminals to Steiner points, such that $(i,j) \in E_1$ when $i \in P$ and $j \in S$.,
        \item [$E_2$] is the set of edges connecting Steiner points, such that $(i,j) \in E_2$ if $i,j \in S$ and $i < j$.
    \end{itemizeb}

    \subsection{Convex Hull Approximation TODO fix the naming}

    We can now use the convex hull condition in a different way by exploiting disjunctive programming~\cite[2.11]{Conforti2014}.
    We have the following:
    \begin{equation}
        x = (x_1, \ldots, x_{|S|}) \in \text{conv}(P \times \ldots \times P)
    \end{equation}

    If we fix each $x$ to be a point in $P$ we can write:
    \begin{align}
        x_i &= P_{a(i)} \\
        w &= b_a y
    \end{align}
    Where $a: S \rightarrow P$ is a function that maps each Steiner point to a terminal and $b_a$ is the matrix of the bilinear
    constraints once $x$ is fixed to an assignment $a$.
    Calling the set of all possible $a$ as $\mathcal{A}$ we can write the problem as:
    \begin{align}
        x &= \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} x_a \\
        w &= \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} w_a \\
        x_s &= \lambda_a P_{a(s)} \\
        w_a &= \lambda_a b_a y \\
        \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \lambda_a &= 1 \\
        \lambda_a &\geq 0
    \end{align}

    We now need to explicitely state $w_a$
    \begin{align}
        w_{a, s,t} &= \lambda_a \cdot s_s \cdot y_{s,t} \quad \forall s \in S, t \in T \\
        w_{a, s,s'} &= \lambda_a \cdot P_{a(s)} \cdot y_{s,s'} \quad \forall s, s' \in S, s < s' \\
        w_{a, s',s} &= \lambda_a \cdot P_{a(s')} \cdot y_{s,s'} \quad \forall s, s' \in S, s < s' \\
    \end{align}

    We can observe that the first equation does not depend on $x$.
